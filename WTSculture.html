Culture of Serbia

Stefan Nemanjic (The first Serbian King)

Serbian culture is a term that encompasses the artistic, culinary, literary, musical, political and social elements that are representative of Serbs and Serbia.

History

Since it initially controlled the Byzantine and Frankish frontiers in the emperors' names, the Byzantine Empire had a significant impact on Serbian culture. Soon after, the Serbs established their own nation. They adopted the Cyrillic character and were baptized by Eastern Orthodox missionaries; in the southern areas, both Latin and Catholic influences were present. The marine areas of the Serbian state were impacted by the Republic of Venice in the Middle Ages. In 1219, Constantinople granted autocephaly to the Serbian Orthodox Church.

The pope proclaimed Stefan the First Crowned king, ushering in Serbian culture's thriving Middle Ages. In 1459, the Ottoman Empire overthrew the Serbian Despotate, bringing an end to a period of political and artistic rebirth. Ottomans dominated the area and had a significant impact on Serbian culture, particularly in the south. At the same time, in the northern areas, the Habsburg monarchy began to encroach on what is now Serbian territory at the end of the 17th century, tying this part of the country culturally to Central Europe rather than the Balkans. Serbia's people began to have the most significant cultural effect after the Serbian Revolution resulted in autonomy and then independence.

Yugoslav Wars

In the post-WWII era, Serbia was a vital part of Yugoslavia, a unified communist state led by Josip Broz Tito. Belgrade, now Serbia's capital, was the federation's capital. In the early 1990s, Croatia, Slovenia, Macedonia, and Bosnia declared independence, while Serbia and Montenegro formed the new Republic of Yugoslavia in 1992. Slobodan Milosevic, former Serbian President, aimed to unite ethnic Serbs in neighboring countries, fueling conflicts and causing substantial loss and damage.

These events led to a significant increase in Serbia's Serb population due to refugees from Croatia and Bosnia. Economic struggles ensued due to Western embargoes and air raids during the Kosovo war in 1999. Many Serbs hold bitterness toward the West, believing they were wrongly portrayed in the media. The scars of the conflicts and blame for the Balkan wars still resonate, hindering reconciliation.

For Serbs, the wars evoke deep sorrow and anger, creating a complex reconciliation process. Displacement, family separation, and fear of military conscription drove many to flee Serbia. Generational differences shape attitudes toward the Yugoslav Wars, with the older generation often finding it difficult to discuss the conflicts openly. They passed on their experiences and historical narratives to the younger generation, who may be more open to dialogue in the hope of reconciliation.

Serbs, more than other groups in the region, identify as Yugoslav, reflecting nostalgia for the prosperous years of unified Yugoslavia, especially during Tito's regime when Serbia enjoyed economic prosperity and a higher standard of living.

The Serbian Orthodox Church was the westernmost outpost of Eastern Orthodox Christianity in Europe, and via interactions with Catholicism and Islam, it helped to define the course of its historical development.

Slava

A Serbian slava is a unique cultural and religious tradition among Serbian Orthodox Christians. Families choose a specific patron saint, who becomes their protector and spiritual guardian. The slava is celebrated on the feast day of this chosen saint, involving a religious service with prayers and blessings performed by a priest in the family's home. After the service, a festive meal is served, featuring traditional Serbian dishes and a special slava cake. The slava is an occasion for family gatherings and inviting friends and neighbors, reinforcing family bonds, preserving Serbian Orthodox heritage, and emphasizing faith and community. It's a deeply significant aspect of Serbian culture, connecting spirituality and cultural identity through family traditions.

'Samo Sloga Srbina Spasava' ('only unity saves the Serbs')

Identity and Pride

The unspoken but widely recognized national slogan, 'samo sloga Srbina spasava' ('only unity saves the Serbs'), embodies the belief that unity is essential for the survival of the Serbian people. This mindset is deeply rooted in Serbia's tumultuous history, particularly the experiences during the Yugoslav Wars, and reflects the collectivist nature of society, where loyalty to friends and family is paramount.

Serbia's historical events significantly contribute to a strong sense of national pride. Serbs often commemorate Prince Lazar's role in the 1389 battle between Christian and Ottoman forces, a symbolic representation of national suffering and a pillar in the formation of Serbian identity. These historical events and myths are passed down through generations, shaping the core of Serbian identity. Artists who fled the conflicts abroad often portrayed Serbian culture in a nationalist light, emphasizing these national myths.

The Serbian identity is closely tied to ethnicity and religious affiliation. The national slogan is depicted as four large 'C' letters within each quarter of a Serbian Orthodox crucifix, a widely recognized symbol seen across Serbia and other Serb-populated regions in the former Yugoslavia, often in graffiti. The connection between Serbian identity and religion is further reinforced by language. The Serbian language, typically written in the Cyrillic alphabet, has its origins in religious liturgy. The Serbian Orthodox faith and language are integral aspects that differentiate Serbia from its neighboring countries.



Humour and ‘Inat’

A common feature of Serbian behavior is humor. Inat is a term for obstinacy and resistance that is frequently used in lighthearted contexts. Many Serbs believe it to be a positive quality that shows fortitude in the face of impossible challenges. One instance of inat is when citizens of Belgrade held rock concerts and wore shirts with target symbols on the backs in protest of the brutal methods NATO used to intervene there in 1999.

Aphorisms and dark humor are also common in Serbia. An aphorism typically consists of one or two short, snappy lines that express a truth about a prevalent societal issue or mental state in a clear, often brutal manner. This brand of humor has recently helped the nation get through the post-war adjustment. Rastko Zakic, a Serbian author, wrote, "We will try not to have any more fratricide," during the Yugoslav Wars.
